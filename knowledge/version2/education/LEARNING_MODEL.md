## 1. Purpose

This document defines what **learning** means inside Kwalitec.

It answers four governing questions:

1. What counts as learning?  
2. What must not be mistaken for learning?  
3. Along which dimensions must learning be pursued and evidenced?  
4. What constitutes educational success?

The Learning Model is binding educational doctrine for Version 2 and beyond. It does not prescribe algorithms, interfaces, or storage. It defines educational meaning.

---

## 2. What Learning Is

Learning, in Kwalitec, is a **durable, multi-dimensional improvement** in a student’s capacity relative to official curriculum aims.

More precisely, learning has occurred when there is reasoned, evidence-supported grounds to believe that the student has advanced in one or more of the following:

1. **Understanding** — deeper conceptual grasp  
2. **Application** — more reliable use of knowledge in tasks  
3. **Connection** — richer linkage among ideas, methods, and syllabus parts  
4. **Retention** — greater persistence of capacity over time  
5. **Transfer** — greater success under legitimate variation and novelty  

Learning is therefore:

- **Directional** — toward learning objectives grounded in the syllabus;  
- **Evidential** — claimed only with observational support;  
- **Cumulative** — built across episodes, not conferred by a single event;  
- **Honest** — stated with uncertainty when evidence is thin.

Kwalitec’s product promise — *reduce decisions, increase learning* — uses this definition of learning. Reducing decisions while increasing only activity, coverage, or engagement would miss the promise.

---

## 3. What Learning Is NOT

The following are **not** learning, though some may accompany it or enable it:

| Non-learning | Why it fails the definition |
|--------------|-------------------------------|
| Time on task alone | Duration does not imply cognitive change |
| Highlighting, watching, or passive exposure | Exposure is not durable capacity change |
| Marking a topic complete | Coverage answers “studied?”, not “learned?” |
| Finishing a mission, session, or checklist | Completion closes work; it does not prove capacity |
| A single correct answer | May reflect recognition, luck, or mimicry |
| Self-declared confidence alone | Feeling is soft signal, not mastery |
| Streaks, points, or engagement metrics | Motivate behaviour; they do not measure understanding |
| Accessing an explanation without uptake | Delivery ≠ acquisition |
| Memorising a formula without conditions of use | Fragile performance under exam rewording |

**Governing sentence:** Activity can be necessary for learning; activity is never sufficient to claim learning.

## 4. Five Dimensions of Learning

Kwalitec analyses learning along five dimensions. Educational design, tutoring, and success criteria must attend to all five over time. A platform that maximises only one dimension (for example short-term application score) produces distorted preparation.

```text
Understanding
Application
Connection
Retention
Transfer
```

These dimensions are analytically distinct and educationally interdependent. Progress on one may support another; none may be treated as a proxy that erases the others.

---

### 4.1 Understanding

**Meaning**  
The student grasps meanings, principles, and relationships — and can explain them — rather than only recognising familiar cues.

**Why it matters**  
Professional examinations probe whether candidates know *what* a method is doing and *why* it applies. Without understanding, performance collapses when wording or assumptions shift.

**Observable evidence (illustrative)**  
- Accurate explanation in the student’s own words  
- Correct identification of underlying principle in a mixed set  
- Ability to state conditions under which a method is valid  
- Detection of an invalid approach and reason for invalidity  
- Coherent answers to “why” and “what if” probes  

**Weak or insufficient alone**  
- Selecting a correct multiple-choice option without explanation  
- Reciting a definition verbatim with no relational grasp  

**Actuarial example**  
Explaining why prospective and retrospective reserves agree under stated assumptions — not only computing one number from a template.

---

### 4.2 Application

**Meaning**  
The student can use knowledge to perform legitimate tasks: calculate, classify, derive, or decide within the syllabus’s demand.

**Why it matters**  
Examinations award marks for correct, methodical application under constraints. Understanding that never becomes performance is incomplete preparation; performance without understanding is brittle.

**Observable evidence (illustrative)**  
- Correct solutions on standard and mildly varied exercises  
- Appropriate method selection for a stated problem  
- Accurate execution of multi-step procedures  
- Recovery from a partial result to a complete solution  

**Weak or insufficient alone**  
- Success only when the problem matches a drilled template exactly  
- Correct final answers with systematically wrong reasoning (where reasoning is inspectable)  

**Actuarial example**  
Computing the EPV of a term assurance given mortality and interest bases, with correct handling of timing conventions.

---

### 4.3 Connection

**Meaning**  
The student relates ideas across a topic and across related syllabus areas: linking concepts, seeing shared structure, and avoiding isolated “pigeonhole” knowledge.

**Why it matters**  
Actuarial syllabuses are cumulative. Later topics presuppose earlier ones. Exam questions often combine strands. Isolated facts that do not connect produce fragmented study and surprise under combination questions.

**Observable evidence (illustrative)**  
- Explicit mapping of a new idea to a prior principle  
- Correct use of prerequisites inside a later task  
- Recognition that two different-looking problems share a structure  
- Accurate explanation of how one result implies another  

**Weak or insufficient alone**  
- Memorising adjacent chapter summaries without relational statements  
- Practising topics only in isolation forever  

**Actuarial example**  
Connecting life-table probabilities to assurance EPVs, and those to premium equations — seeing one cash-flow logic rather than three unrelated recipes.

---

### 4.4 Retention

**Meaning**  
The student’s capacity persists across time — including after intentional gaps — rather than existing only in the immediate aftermath of study.

**Why it matters**  
Professional routes stretch over months and years. Peak same-day performance is a seductive false signal. Retention is what remains available near the examination.

**Observable evidence (illustrative)**  
- Successful performance after a meaningful delay without full re-teaching  
- Stable method selection after time away  
- Faster re-acquisition that still demonstrates residual structure (relearning savings), interpreted cautiously  
- Continued accuracy on previously strong objectives in spaced checks  

**Weak or insufficient alone**  
- End-of-episode quiz excellence with no later probe  
- Student claim “I still remember” without performance  

**Actuarial example**  
Correctly handling select mortality in a mock sitting weeks after first study, without reconstructing the concept from scratch.

---

### 4.5 Transfer

**Meaning**  
The student applies knowledge under legitimate novelty: new numbers, new wordings, new combinations, or exam-like constraints that preserve syllabus validity while changing surface form.

**Why it matters**  
Examiners redesign surfaces. Transfer is the educational antidote to template addiction. It is also among the strongest practical indicators that understanding is genuine.

**Observable evidence (illustrative)**  
- Success on unseen variants that remain within syllabus scope  
- Correct adaptation when a familiar assumption is altered  
- Performance under timed or exam-format conditions on familiar objectives  
- Explanation of how a practised method extends to a new stem  

**Weak or insufficient alone**  
- High scores only on near-identical clones of homework  
- Failure whenever interest rate, payment frequency, or wording changes  

**Actuarial example**  
Having practised continuous whole life assurances, correctly tackling a deferred continuous assurance word problem that was never drilled in that exact form.

## 5. How the Dimensions Work Together

| If this is strong… | But this is weak… | Educational risk |
|--------------------|-------------------|------------------|
| Understanding | Application | Insight without exam performance |
| Application | Understanding | Brittle mimicry; collapse under rewording |
| Application | Retention | Short-lived peaks; exam-day fade |
| Understanding + Application | Transfer | Textbook competence, exam fragility |
| All short-term dimensions | Connection | Siloed topics; combination-question failure |

**Tutor implication:** diagnosis should name which dimension is the bottleneck. Teaching strategy should target that dimension rather than defaulting to “more questions.”

**Mastery implication:** mastery-consistent belief requires converging evidence across dimensions over time — especially retention and transfer — not a single high application score.

## 6. Learning Episodes and Dimensional Progress

Every Learning Episode should be designed to advance **at least one** learning dimension in a diagnosable way — and, under Educational Atomicity, to pursue **exactly one** deliberate educational purpose (one capability improvement).

That requirement does not mean every episode advances all five dimensions. It means:

1. The episode has an intentional dimensional aim (typically one primary dimension);  
2. The episode has exactly one teaching goal;  
3. Evidence is sought that is relevant to that aim;  
4. Closing the episode without any dimensional educational purpose is forbidden as empty work theatre.

Over a journey through a topic, all five dimensions should receive attention appropriate to the subject and the student’s state — composed as sequences of atomic episodes, not as one multi-purpose blob.

## 7. Educational Success

### 7.1 Definition

**Educational success** in Kwalitec is the state in which, for relevant learning objectives, there is sufficient accumulated evidence that the student has advanced — and where appropriate, sustained — capacity across the five dimensions, such that estimated readiness for syllabus-valid assessment is honest and improving.

Educational success is **not**:

- finishing the plan;  
- maintaining a streak;  
- consuming all content;  
- maximising minutes studied;  
- scoring well once.

### 7.2 Markers of success (qualitative)

1. **Dimensional breadth** — progress is not confined to a single dimension.  
2. **Evidential depth** — claims rest on multiple, appropriately strong evidence sources.  
3. **Temporal honesty** — retention has been probed where mastery is asserted.  
4. **Transfer honesty** — novelty within syllabus scope has been faced where mastery is asserted.  
5. **Misconception hygiene** — known misconceptions have been addressed explicitly.  
6. **Explainable next steps** — further work is justified by educational diagnosis, not novelty-seeking.

### 7.3 Success relative to examinations

Kwalitec’s ultimate practical horizon is professional examination performance. The Learning Model treats exam success as downstream of dimensional learning — never as a reason to replace learning with cram theatre. Short-term mark gains that sacrifice understanding, retention, or transfer are not educational success under this model.

## 9. Summary Propositions

1. Learning is durable, multi-dimensional capacity change supported by evidence.  
2. Completion, time, and single correct answers are not learning.  
3. The five dimensions — Understanding, Application, Connection, Retention, Transfer — jointly define what Kwalitec must cultivate.  
4. Each dimension has distinct observable evidence.  
5. Educational success is honest, evidence-based advancement toward exam-credible capacity — not the closing of tasks.
