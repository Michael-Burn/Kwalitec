## 1. Purpose

This document defines how authors encode **application** and **transfer** — authentic uses of concepts under syllabus-legitimate demand.

It answers:

> **Where and how should knowledge be used — and what variation proves flexible understanding?**

This is educational architecture for authoring contexts. It is not an item bank, not exam coaching tips, and not a difficulty heuristic alone.

---

## 2. Governing Distinctions

| Term | Meaning |
|------|---------|
| **Application** | Using knowledge to perform legitimate syllabus tasks (calculate, classify, derive, decide, explain in use) |
| **Application Context** | A situation *type* characterised by task demands, assumptions, constraints, and typical surface forms |
| **Transfer** | Successful use when surface features differ from those practised, while underlying structure remains the same |
| **Transfer Context** | An application situation designed with controlled surface variation |
| **Clone competence** | Success only on near-identical surface forms — weak evidence of understanding |

### Governing sentences

> Application follows understanding.  
> Practice without conceptual anchoring produces ritual competence.  
> Transfer requires multiple contexts.  
> Authenticity means syllabus-legitimate and professionally sensible use — not theatrical realia.

---

## 3. Application Contexts

### 3.1 Definition

An **Application Context** is a reusable description of a situation type in which one or more concepts/skills are legitimately exercised.

It is not a single exam question. Many items may instantiate one context.

### 3.2 What Authors Capture

| Field | Content |
|-------|---------|
| **Task demand** | What the student must do (compute, classify, derive, decide, interpret) |
| **Knowledge targets** | Concepts, skills, procedures, principles involved |
| **Assumptions** | Mortality basis, interest, expenses, independence, etc., as relevant |
| **Constraints** | Time pressure class, permitted tools, required exactness |
| **Surface forms** | Typical wordings, notations, tables, diagrams |
| **Success indicators** | What correct use looks like (beyond a final number) |
| **Common failure modes** | Including links to authored misconceptions where patterned |

### 3.3 Educational Role

Application contexts convert meaning into competence. They host examples and procedures. Narrow contexts alone produce clone competence; varied contexts prepare transfer.

---

## 4. Transfer Contexts

### 4.1 Definition

A **Transfer Context** is an application situation that differs in surface features from those already practised, while remaining within legitimate subject demand and the same underlying conceptual structure (unless the author is deliberately introducing a *new* objective — which must be labelled as such).

### 4.2 What Authors Capture

| Field | Content |
|-------|---------|
| **Base context(s)** | Which application contexts this varies from |
| **What varies** | Wording, numbers, contract features, representation, combination with neighbouring concepts |
| **What stays constant** | Target structure / principle / procedure class |
| **Transfer distance** | Near or far (see below) |
| **Objective alignment** | Same learning objective vs boundary of a new objective |
| **Evidence value** | What success/failure implies for understanding and mastery claims |

### 4.3 Educational Role

Transfer contexts test structure vs template. They are strong evidence toward mastery when combined with retention and explanation — never proved by one lucky success.

## 5. Near Transfer and Far Transfer

### 5.1 Near Transfer

**Definition**  
Surface variation is modest: numbers, light rewording, isomorphic structure, same representation family, same concept cluster.

**When to author**  
Early after initial application success; as a check against pure clone lock before claiming competence.

**Risk**  
Treating near transfer as mastery; authors stopping here when examinations demand recombination.

---

### 5.2 Far Transfer

**Definition**  
Surface variation is substantial: novel wording, different representation, recombination with other syllabus concepts, altered constraints — while the underlying demand remains in scope for the objective (or is explicitly escalated to a new objective).

**When to author**  
After near transfer is available; for objectives that claim flexible understanding or exam-combination readiness.

**Risk**  
Far variation that secretly introduces a new concept without naming a new objective; unfair “gotchas” outside syllabus legitimacy.

---

### 5.3 Design Judgement

The boundary between near and far — and between far transfer and a **new learning objective** — is a design judgement guided by:

- Named knowledge targets  
- Syllabus scope  
- Whether success requires a concept not yet in the objective’s concept set  

Authors must record the judgement. There is no universal numeric distance metric in this architecture (consistent with Subject Knowledge Model ambiguity on transfer thresholds).

## 6. Application Families (Authoring Lenses)

Authors should encode authentic uses through the following lenses. A single context may serve more than one lens; lenses must not be confused.

### 6.1 Professional Application

**What it is**  
Use that resembles legitimate professional reasoning in the discipline: advising, valuing, modelling, classifying risk, interpreting results under assumptions.

**Authoring guidance**

- Preserve professional sense-making (why the calculation matters)  
- Keep assumptions explicit  
- Avoid cartoon scenarios that teach the wrong structure  
- Do not require workplace software or proprietary firm methods as core  

**Illustration**  
Interpreting a net premium as the premium that equates EPVs of benefits and premiums under stated bases — professional meaning, not only algebraic rearrangement.

---

### 6.2 Exam Application

**What it is**  
Use aligned with how professional examinations legitimately elicit the objective: timed conditions, characteristic wordings, multi-part structures, required presentation of working.

**Authoring guidance**

- Reflect examination *forms of demand* without copying proprietary items  
- Include combination and rewording patterns the syllabus makes fair game  
- Separate exam application from “exam tricks” that bypass understanding  

**Illustration**  
A multi-part question that first asks for classification of contract type, then for a net premium under stated tables — exam-shaped application of authored concepts.

---

### 6.3 Real-World Application

**What it is**  
Use situated in everyday or societal settings that still preserve correct subject structure (e.g. insurance consumer narrative, public risk illustration) when helpful for meaning.

**Authoring guidance**

- Real-world colour must not falsify the model  
- Prefer clarity of structure over elaborate story  
- Mark clearly when a real-world frame is motivational/intelligibility aid vs examinable demand  

**Illustration**  
A simplified narrative of paying premiums for a life assurance used to motivate cash-flow timing — later retired for precise EPV reasoning.

## 7. How Authors Should Encode Authentic Uses

### 7.1 Principles

1. **Start from concept and objective** — contexts exist to exercise named knowledge, not to fill a practice quota.  
2. **Application before transfer** for the same aim — transfer contexts presuppose application contexts.  
3. **Multiple contexts for transfer claims** — one context cannot underwrite a transfer aim.  
4. **Declare authenticity type** — professional / exam / real-world (or blend) so tutors do not confuse motivational stories with exam demand.  
5. **Keep legitimacy** — all core contexts remain inside syllabus authority.  
6. **Link misconceptions** — where patterned failure is expected, point to authored misconceptions.  
7. **Prefer structural fidelity** over decorative realism.

### 7.2 Minimal Sets

For a teachable concept with an application-bearing learning objective, authors should provide at least:

| Artefact | Minimum |
|----------|---------|
| Application contexts | One canonical context; preferably two with modest variation |
| Transfer contexts | At least one near-transfer context before claiming transfer readiness; far transfer when the objective demands it |
| Exam-shaped demand | At least one exam application lens where the syllabus is examination-oriented |
| Professional sense | Explicit interpretive demand somewhere in the set when principles are in scope |

### 7.3 Ordering Relative to Understanding

Educational sequence for the learner:

```text
Understanding (meaning, boundaries)
        ↓
Application (legitimate use)
        ↓
Near transfer
        ↓
Far transfer / recombination (as objective allows)
```

Authoring designs the whole ladder once concepts exist; teaching must not skip understanding for drill (Authoring Invariants; Educational Invariants).

## 8. Relationship to Assessment and Mastery

- **Assessment opportunities** often instantiate application or transfer contexts.  
- Success in application contexts supports competence claims.  
- Success in transfer contexts supports stronger understanding and mastery claims when converging with explanation and retention.  
- Clone-only success must not be authored as sufficient mastery evidence.

## 9. Relationship to Digital Twin and Episodes

| Consumer | Use of authored contexts |
|----------|--------------------------|
| Learning Episodes | Select contexts matching intention (practice, transfer probe, repair) |
| Teaching Strategies | May specify variation / transfer as the instructional approach |
| Digital Twin | Interprets performance *in* contexts against knowledge targets — does not invent contexts |

## 10. Architectural Ambiguities (Declared)

1. **Near/far boundary** — qualitative; must be recorded per transfer context.  
2. **Far transfer vs new objective** — recombination may create a new aim; authors must not silently expand scope.  
3. **Authenticity vs fairness** — real-world framing can introduce cultural or linguistic load unrelated to the concept; keep structural priority.  
4. **Exam application without past papers** — authors encode demand *patterns*, not copyrighted items.

---

## 11. Non-Goals

This model does not:

- Publish examination questions  
- Define adaptive difficulty algorithms  
- Replace Learning Model dimensions of Application and Transfer  
- Specify item formats (MCQ, written, etc.) as architecture — format is a later content decision serving the context  

---

## 12. Summary

Authors encode authentic uses by defining application contexts and transfer contexts across professional, exam, and real-world lenses; distinguishing near from far transfer; requiring multiple contexts before transfer claims; and keeping application after understanding — so competence is structural, not cloned.
