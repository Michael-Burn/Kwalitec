## 1. Purpose

This document catalogues the **named instructional strategies** available to the Kwalitec Version 2 tutor.

Each entry is an educational reasoning artefact: a legitimate *how* for pursuing a Teaching Intention. Entries are not UI templates, prompt libraries, lesson scripts, or content files.

Episode types refer to [`LEARNING_EPISODE_TYPES.md`](LEARNING_EPISODE_TYPES.md). Intentions refer to [`TEACHING_INTENTION_MODEL.md`](TEACHING_INTENTION_MODEL.md).

---

## 2. Catalogue Principles

1. **Named and justifiable** — strategies are selected by educational fit, not fashion.  
2. **Intention-serving** — every use cites a Teaching Intention.  
3. **Evidence-expectant** — each strategy defines what evidence it should produce.  
4. **Non-exclusive** — specialised variants remain lawful when justified.  
5. **Implementation-free** — definitions never prescribe screens, prompts, or storage.

### 3.1 Direct Explanation

**Definition**  
The tutor presents a clear, structured account of the target idea or method — meanings, conditions of validity, and key distinctions — before demanding independent performance.

**Educational purpose**  
Establish or restore a correct explanatory frame when absence or thin understanding is the primary problem.

**When appropriate**  
Cold start or near-absence of the concept; student lacks a usable model to discover from; cognitive load makes unguided exploration inefficient; a precise definitional frame is required before practice.

**When inappropriate**  
Stable misconception that will assimilate explanation into the wrong model without confrontation; student already explains adequately and needs application or transfer; false confidence that will dismiss explanation without discrimination.

**Learning intentions best served**  
Build intuition; Consolidate understanding (thin facets); Strengthen prerequisite (conceptual upstream gaps).

**Typical episode types**  
Concept Introduction; Concept Deepening; Prerequisite Repair.

**Expected evidence**  
Own-words paraphrase; identification of conditions of validity; correct rejection of a simple invalid use; reduced “formula only” talk on introductory probes.

**Advantages**  
Efficient under thin prior knowledge; creates shared vocabulary; reduces floundering.

**Limitations**  
Passive exposure is weak without interaction; may create illusion of understanding; insufficient alone for misconception repair or transfer.

**Prerequisites**  
Curriculum prerequisites for symbols and upstream meaning, or concurrent Prerequisite Repair for critical gaps only.

### 3.2 Analogy

**Definition**  
The tutor maps the target idea onto a more familiar structure so the student can borrow relational understanding, then explicitly marks where the analogy holds and where it fails.

**Educational purpose**  
Build intuition and relational grasp when formal presentation alone is opaque.

**When appropriate**  
Student has a usable source domain; target is abstract or notation-heavy; diagnosis shows recognition without felt sense of *why*.

**When inappropriate**  
No safe source domain; analogy risks reinforcing a known misconception; student already needs discrimination among near concepts more than metaphor; exam-horizon work that requires formal precision without time to unwind analogy residue.

**Learning intentions best served**  
Build intuition; Consolidate understanding; Connect fragmented knowledge (when analogy links syllabus nodes carefully).

**Typical episode types**  
Concept Introduction; Concept Deepening; Concept Connection.

**Expected evidence**  
Student can state the mapping; identify a boundary where the analogy breaks; apply the target idea in a simple formal probe without relying on the metaphor alone.

**Advantages**  
Reduces abstraction barrier; memorable; supports first explanatory power.

**Limitations**  
Over-extension is common; analogy residue can distort later formal use; weak as sole strategy for procedural fluency.

**Prerequisites**  
Adequate familiarity with the source domain; minimal target vocabulary to receive the mapping.

---

### 3.3 Worked Example

**Definition**  
The tutor presents a complete, annotated solution path so the student studies legitimate method structure before independent demand — including paused or completion variants that require prediction of next steps.

**Educational purpose**  
Make a method intelligible and reduce cognitive load before guided or independent practice.

**When appropriate**  
New procedure; high intrinsic load; diagnosis indicates modelling need before practice; planned fade toward Guided Practice.

**When inappropriate**  
Student already fluent and only needs retrieval or timing; misconception that will copy wrong “reason” embedded in uncritical reading; sole strategy when transfer under variation is the intention.

**Learning intentions best served**  
Build intuition (method sense); Increase procedural fluency (entry); Strengthen application; Strengthen prerequisite (procedural upstream).

**Typical episode types**  
Worked Example; Concept Introduction (method-framed); Guided Practice (immediate follow-on).

**Expected evidence**  
Student narrates why steps are taken; predicts a paused next step; identifies a critical decision point in the example.

**Advantages**  
Strong for novices; reduces floundering; supports schema formation.

**Limitations**  
Copying blindness; expertise reversal if overused with advanced students; does not alone prove transfer.

**Prerequisites**  
Concept floor for symbols and meaning; or concurrent minimal concept cue.

---

### 3.4 Guided Discovery

**Definition**  
The tutor designs constrained inquiry so the student constructs key relationships through prompted exploration, with scaffolds that keep discovery educationally productive rather than aimless.

**Educational purpose**  
Deepen understanding and ownership of relationships when the student has enough prior structure to discover without drowning.

**When appropriate**  
Partial understanding present; student can manipulate examples; intention is insight or boundary grasp rather than first exposure under total absence.

**When inappropriate**  
Near-zero prior knowledge (prefer Direct Explanation / Worked Example); severe time pressure with no room for productive struggle; open discovery that violates atomicity by spanning many concepts.

**Learning intentions best served**  
Build intuition; Consolidate understanding; Complete missing facets; Improve transfer (when discovery varies surface forms carefully).

**Typical episode types**  
Concept Deepening; Concept Connection; Guided Practice (inquiry-framed).

**Expected evidence**  
Student states the discovered relationship in own words; applies it to a near-transfer probe; articulates a condition of validity found through the inquiry.

**Advantages**  
Stronger encoding; reveals misconceptions during construction; supports durable understanding.

**Limitations**  
Time-costly; risk of unproductive struggle; may under-serve students who need explicit frames first.

**Prerequisites**  
Enough prior grasp to explore; clear inquiry boundaries protecting Educational Atomicity.

---

### 3.5 Socratic Questioning

**Definition**  
The tutor advances learning primarily through sequenced questions that force discrimination, justification, and revision of the student’s own claims.

**Educational purpose**  
Surface and remodel thinking — especially incomplete understanding and fragile explanations — without replacing student reasoning with tutor monologue.

**When appropriate**  
Student can produce some explanation; diagnosis shows thin, contradictory, or overconfident verbal understanding; intention includes calibration or consolidation through articulation.

**When inappropriate**  
Student lacks any foothold to answer from (need Direct Explanation first); pure fluency drills; high-anxiety contexts where questioning without teaching opportunity becomes interrogation theatre.

**Learning intentions best served**  
Consolidate understanding; Repair misconception (as probe layer); Calibrate confidence downward; Recover confidence (gentle success-visible questioning when capacity exists).

**Typical episode types**  
Concept Deepening; Misconception Repair; Confidence Calibration; Reflection.

**Expected evidence**  
Improved justifications; student revises an earlier claim; correct discrimination under contrastive questions; better self-appraisal language.

**Advantages**  
Makes thinking visible; excellent for diagnosis refinement; resists passive illusion of understanding.

**Limitations**  
Can stall without intermittent teaching moves; culturally or affectively harsh if misused; slow for pure skill acquisition.

**Prerequisites**  
Minimal content foothold; psychological safety for productive questioning.

---

### 3.6 Retrieval First

**Definition**  
The tutor elicits retrieval of target knowledge *before* re-teaching, using the attempt (successful or failed) as the instructional opening.

**Educational purpose**  
Strengthen memory and diagnose residual grasp; prepare encoding by activating prior traces.

**When appropriate**  
Prior exposure exists; intention includes retention or consolidation; evidence history shows fade risk; exam horizon with previously taught material.

**When inappropriate**  
Genuine cold start with nothing to retrieve; severe misconception where failed retrieval will only rehearse the wrong model without immediate confrontation plan; student in acute confidence collapse where initial failure would be educationally cruel without scaffolds.

**Learning intentions best served**  
Improve retention; Consolidate understanding; Prepare for examination (as activation, not sole exam method).

**Typical episode types**  
Retrieval / Fluency; Spaced Retrieval; Mixed Review; Confidence Calibration.

**Expected evidence**  
Retrieval success rate and quality; error patterns that refine diagnosis; subsequent improved retention on delayed probes.

**Advantages**  
Potent for memory; reveals true residual knowledge; efficient use of prior teaching investment.

**Limitations**  
Failed retrieval without follow-up teaching wastes the opening; can demoralise if overused; not a substitute for misconception repair.

**Prerequisites**  
Prior instructional opportunity on the objective (or lawful residual exposure).

---

### 3.7 Retrieval After Instruction

**Definition**  
The tutor teaches (or re-teaches) first, then requires retrieval of the taught content after a short or planned delay within or across episodes — not mere immediate echo.

**Educational purpose**  
Consolidate newly taught material into memory and check that instruction produced retrievable knowledge.

**When appropriate**  
After Direct Explanation, Worked Example, or repair; intention includes retention of what was just established; spacing plan exists or is implied.

**When inappropriate**  
As the only move when understanding was never established; immediate verbatim repeat that tests short-term echo rather than retrieval; when transfer, not retention, is the primary unsolved problem.

**Learning intentions best served**  
Improve retention; Consolidate understanding; Increase procedural fluency (method steps as retrievable structure).

**Typical episode types**  
Retrieval / Fluency; Spaced Retrieval; Concept Deepening (close with retrieval); Guided Practice (retrieve principle mid-practice).

**Expected evidence**  
Successful delayed recall of key relations or steps; reduced dependence on looking back; maintained accuracy after brief delay.

**Advantages**  
Converts teaching into durable knowledge; detects illusory understanding early.

**Limitations**  
Too-early retrieval can fail noisily; over-spacing before stability wastes teaching; does not alone create transfer.

**Prerequisites**  
A fair instructional opportunity on the target before the retrieval demand.

---

### 3.8 Concept Comparison

**Definition**  
The tutor places two or more related ideas side by side and forces explicit discrimination of similarities, differences, and conditions of use.

**Educational purpose**  
Reduce confusion among near concepts and build relational understanding.

**When appropriate**  
Knowledge fragmentation; recurring mix-ups between neighbouring syllabus ideas; transfer failures driven by surface-feature confusion.

**When inappropriate**  
One concept is not yet introduced at all; comparison spans unrelated objectives violating atomicity; student needs prerequisite repair on one node before comparison is honest.

**Learning intentions best served**  
Connect fragmented knowledge; Consolidate understanding; Improve transfer; Repair misconception (when misconception is category confusion).

**Typical episode types**  
Concept Connection; Concept Deepening; Misconception Repair; Interleaved Practice.

**Expected evidence**  
Correct sorting of cases; explicit difference statements; correct method/concept choice under ambiguous stems.

**Advantages**  
Directly trains discrimination; high value for professional syllabi dense with near concepts.

**Limitations**  
Cognitive load if too many nodes; premature comparison confuses; may need prior single-concept floor.

**Prerequisites**  
Introduction-level grasp of each node being compared (or one node newly introduced with the other already stable).

---

### 3.9 Counterexample

**Definition**  
The tutor presents a carefully chosen case that falsifies the student’s current rule or generalization, then rebuilds a corrected rule.

**Educational purpose**  
Destabilise an over-general or wrong rule and force refinement of understanding.

**When appropriate**  
Student holds a crisp wrong rule; boundary conditions are the educational target; hypothesis points to over-generalisation.

**When inappropriate**  
Student has no rule to falsify (need explanation first); counterexamples flood without reconstruction; used as gotcha theatre without teaching opportunity.

**Learning intentions best served**  
Repair misconception; Consolidate understanding; Complete missing facets (boundary facets).

**Typical episode types**  
Misconception Repair; Concept Deepening; Error Analysis.

**Expected evidence**  
Student abandons or restricts the old rule; states a corrected condition; succeeds on a new boundary probe.

**Advantages**  
Sharp, memorable; excellent for boundary learning; pairs well with misconception confrontation.

**Limitations**  
Alone may leave a vacuum; weak students may dismiss the case as anomaly; requires precise case design.

**Prerequisites**  
An articulated or elicitible student rule; capacity to engage the falsifying case.

---

### 3.10 Misconception Confrontation

**Definition**  
The tutor explicitly names the wrong mental model, contrasts it with the correct structure, and requires the student to use the corrected model on cases that previously triggered the misconception.

**Educational purpose**  
Displace a stable wrong model rather than hoping volume will overwrite it.

**When appropriate**  
Patterned error indicates a stable misconception; hypothesis names the wrong model; intention is Repair misconception.

**When inappropriate**  
Errors are slips or load failures without a stable wrong model; confrontation without contrast and practice; treating incomplete understanding as misconception.

**Learning intentions best served**  
Repair misconception; Consolidate understanding (post-repair stabilisation as follow-on).

**Typical episode types**  
Misconception Repair; Error Analysis; Concept Deepening; Transfer (post-repair check).

**Expected evidence**  
Correct discrimination on contrastive cases that previously failed; corrected own-words explanation; reduced patterned error on targeted probes.

**Advantages**  
Matches educational ethics of explicit repair; interpretable evidence; prevents drill-on-wrong-model.

**Limitations**  
Affectively demanding; mis-diagnosis of slips as misconceptions wastes confrontation; requires well-designed contrasts.

**Prerequisites**  
Warranted misconception diagnosis; contrastive materials; fair instructional opportunity to install the correct model.

---

### 3.11 Progressive Scaffolding

**Definition**  
The tutor provides structured supports (hints, subgoals, representations, checklists) that are rich at first and planned to diminish as competence appears.

**Educational purpose**  
Enable successful application while building toward independence without permanent crutch dependence.

**When appropriate**  
After modelling; student not ready for full independence; intention is strengthen application or procedural fluency under support.

**When inappropriate**  
Scaffolds that never fade; scaffolds that hide the conceptual structure; independent-capable students who need retrieval or transfer instead.

**Learning intentions best served**  
Strengthen application; Increase procedural fluency; Strengthen prerequisite (procedural); Recover confidence (success-visible supports when capacity exists).

**Typical episode types**  
Guided Practice; Prerequisite Repair; Recovery.

**Expected evidence**  
Improving solutions with scaffold use; decreasing hint dependence; partial then fuller justifications.

**Advantages**  
Keeps practice in the productive zone; visible progress; natural bridge to fading.

**Limitations**  
Scaffold addiction if exit conditions ignored; poor scaffolds teach tricks not structure.

**Prerequisites**  
A modelled or explained method structure; clear fade plan.

---

### 3.12 Faded Guidance

**Definition**  
The tutor deliberately withdraws instructional support across items or episodes — from full modelling toward independence — as understanding and accuracy increase.

**Educational purpose**  
Convert supported performance into independent capability without abrupt abandonment.

**When appropriate**  
Evidence shows rising competence under scaffolds; intention advances from guided to independent application; composition follows Worked Example / Guided Practice.

**When inappropriate**  
Fading while misconception or prerequisite gap remains primary; fading for schedule convenience despite rising errors; abrupt removal that becomes assessment theatre.

**Learning intentions best served**  
Strengthen application; Increase procedural fluency; Improve transfer (fade plus variation).

**Typical episode types**  
Guided Practice; Independent Practice; Worked Example (completion fading).

**Expected evidence**  
Maintained or improving accuracy as supports decrease; student self-initiates steps previously scaffolded.

**Advantages**  
Operationalises “guidance fades over time”; clear progression; interpretable readiness signals.

**Limitations**  
Requires monitoring; premature fade reintroduces floundering; late fade delays independence.

**Prerequisites**  
Prior supported success; intention still application/fluency oriented.

---

### 3.13 Deliberate Practice

**Definition**  
The tutor designs focused, feedback-rich practice on a narrow, weak facet of performance — not undifferentiated volume — with immediate educational feedback and repetition at the edge of competence.

**Educational purpose**  
Improve specific procedural or application weaknesses efficiently.

**When appropriate**  
Concept is adequate; diagnosis isolates a narrow execution or application facet; intention is fluency or application strength.

**When inappropriate**  
Practice on top of misconception; “deliberate” label on random question dumps; practice that never targets the diagnosed facet.

**Learning intentions best served**  
Increase procedural fluency; Strengthen application; Prepare for examination (targeted weak facets only).

**Typical episode types**  
Guided Practice; Independent Practice; Retrieval / Fluency; Timed Practice (when speed facet is warranted).

**Expected evidence**  
Reduced error rate on the targeted facet; improved consistency; retained accuracy under mild pressure when intended.

**Advantages**  
High efficiency; honest targeting; resists coverage theatre.

**Limitations**  
Narrowness can miss relational needs; demotivating if feedback is purely punitive; does not create conceptual understanding from nothing.

**Prerequisites**  
Conceptual warrant for the method; clear facet definition protecting atomicity.

---

### 3.14 Interleaving

**Definition**  
The tutor mixes distinct but related problem types or concepts within a practice sequence so the student must discriminate which knowledge applies, rather than exploiting blocked predictability.

**Educational purpose**  
Improve discrimination and transfer by forcing selection among neighbouring methods or ideas.

**When appropriate**  
Nodes individually practised; mix-ups persist; intention is transfer or connection; exam papers inherently interleave.

**When inappropriate**  
First exposure to all mixed nodes; unresolved misconception on one node; interleaving so dense that load destroys learning; used to “cover more” without discrimination aim.

**Learning intentions best served**  
Improve transfer; Connect fragmented knowledge; Prepare for examination; Consolidate understanding (discrimination facet).

**Typical episode types**  
Interleaved Practice; Mixed Review; Exam Application; Transfer.

**Expected evidence**  
Correct strategy/concept selection under mixed stems; fewer category errors; maintained accuracy relative to blocked practice baseline over time.

**Advantages**  
Strong for durable discrimination; exam-realistic; reveals shallow blocked competence.

**Limitations**  
Harder short-term performance may be misread as failure; overload risk; requires prior single-node competence floor.

**Prerequisites**  
Introduction-level (or better) competence on each interleaved component.

---

### 3.15 Spaced Reinforcement

**Definition**  
The tutor revisits a previously taught objective after planned delays, with retrieval and brief re-teaching as needed, to strengthen retention across time.

**Educational purpose**  
Improve durability of learning against forgetting.

**When appropriate**  
Prior acquisition occurred; evidence shows fade; exam horizon makes retention critical; intention is improve retention.

**When inappropriate**  
Spacing material never successfully taught; spacing used to avoid repairing a live misconception; cramming labelled as spacing.

**Learning intentions best served**  
Improve retention; Consolidate understanding; Prepare for examination (retention component).

**Typical episode types**  
Spaced Retrieval; Mixed Review; Retrieval / Fluency; Reflection (metacognitive spacing plan).

**Expected evidence**  
Improved delayed retrieval; reduced re-teaching need over successive spacings; stable performance after longer intervals.

**Advantages**  
Strong evidence base for durability; efficient long-horizon preparation; complements initial teaching.

**Limitations**  
Requires memory of what was taught; poorly timed spacing yields thrash; does not replace understanding work.

**Prerequisites**  
A prior successful (or partially successful) instructional episode on the objective.

---

### 3.16 Dual Representation

**Definition**  
The tutor presents the same idea in two coordinated representational forms (for example verbal and symbolic; tabular and graphical; narrative stem and formula) and requires translation between them.

**Educational purpose**  
Strengthen understanding and flexibility by binding multiple legitimate representations of one capability.

**When appropriate**  
Student succeeds in one form and fails in another; syllabus demands multi-representational competence; intention is consolidate or transfer across forms.

**When inappropriate**  
Neither representation is grounded yet; dual presentation without linking tasks (parallel noise); load exceeds capacity.

**Learning intentions best served**  
Consolidate understanding; Improve transfer; Build intuition; Strengthen application.

**Typical episode types**  
Concept Deepening; Concept Connection; Transfer; Worked Example (multi-rep annotated).

**Expected evidence**  
Successful translation between forms; correct use of either form on probes; explanation that refers to both representations coherently.

**Advantages**  
Reduces representation-bound fragility; high professional relevance; supports deeper schema.

**Limitations**  
Cognitive load; weak linking yields two siloed half-understandings; design-costly.

**Prerequisites**  
Minimal literacy in each representation, or staged introduction of the second after the first is stable.

---

### 3.17 Concept Mapping

**Definition**  
The tutor requires the student to externalise relationships among a small set of syllabus ideas — hierarchy, conditions, and links — as an explicit relational structure.

**Educational purpose**  
Combat knowledge fragmentation and make relational understanding inspectable.

**When appropriate**  
Fragmentation diagnosis; upcoming synthesis demands; intention is connect fragmented knowledge or consolidate relational facets.

**When inappropriate**  
Map spans an entire chapter (non-atomic); student lacks node-level grasp; mapping becomes decorative busywork without probes.

**Learning intentions best served**  
Connect fragmented knowledge; Consolidate understanding; Improve transfer (relational transfer).

**Typical episode types**  
Concept Connection; Capstone (small-scope); Reflection; Mixed Review (map then apply).

**Expected evidence**  
Accurate links and conditions on the map; use of a mapped relation in a subsequent task; correction of a previously missing link.

**Advantages**  
Makes structure visible; excellent reflection partner; diagnoses fragmentation cleanly.

**Limitations**  
Can become artefact production without learning; scope creep; weak for pure fluency aims.

**Prerequisites**  
Introduction-level grasp of nodes included; tightly bounded map scope.

---

### 3.18 Error-Led Teaching

**Definition**  
The tutor uses a specific student error (or a designed common error) as the instructional centrepiece: analyse what went wrong, why it was tempting, and how the correct path differs.

**Educational purpose**  
Convert error into diagnostic and instructional fuel rather than mere score decrement.

**When appropriate**  
Informative errors available; intention includes repair, consolidation, or application correction; hypothesis can be tested against the error structure.

**When inappropriate**  
Random noise errors with no pattern; public shaming dynamics; error analysis that never returns to correct performance.

**Learning intentions best served**  
Repair misconception; Strengthen application; Consolidate understanding; Calibrate confidence.

**Typical episode types**  
Error Analysis; Misconception Repair; Guided Practice; Reflection.

**Expected evidence**  
Student explains the error mechanism; avoids the same error on a near case; improved justification at the former failure point.

**Advantages**  
Highly targeted; respects evidence; powerful for patterned mistakes.

**Limitations**  
Overfitting to one item; affectively sensitive; insufficient alone if prerequisites are missing.

**Prerequisites**  
An interpretable error artefact; enough content foothold to analyse it.

---

### 3.19 Think-Aloud Modelling

**Definition**  
The tutor externalises expert reasoning while solving or explaining — including decision points, dead ends avoided, and self-monitoring — so the student observes cognitive strategy, not only final form.

**Educational purpose**  
Teach how to think through the task class: monitoring, choice, and justification patterns.

**When appropriate**  
Students produce answers without strategy; exam technique weakness in reading and planning; intention includes application strength or exam preparation with honesty.

**When inappropriate**  
Monologue without student participation; modelling that hides key decisions; used instead of misconception confrontation when a wrong model is stable.

**Learning intentions best served**  
Strengthen application; Prepare for examination; Increase procedural fluency (strategic layer); Recover confidence (transparent manageable process).

**Typical episode types**  
Worked Example; Exam Application; Guided Practice; Timed Practice (planning think-aloud).

**Expected evidence**  
Student imitates decision checkpoints; improved planning before calculation; better error detection in own work.

**Advantages**  
Makes invisible expertise visible; strong for strategy deficits; supports metacognition.

**Limitations**  
Passive watching risk; expert fluency may still be too fast; does not replace practice.

**Prerequisites**  
Content floor sufficient to follow the modelled decisions.

---

### 3.20 Exam Simulation

**Definition**  
The tutor recreates examination-like constraints — timing, paper structure, stem discipline, presentation standards — to train deployment of existing capacity under exam conditions.

**Educational purpose**  
Improve exam-credible performance without substituting for missing conceptual foundations.

**When appropriate**  
Knowledge adequate or just-taught facets stable; intention is Prepare for examination; evidence shows untimed competence with timed collapse, misreads, or allocation failure.

**When inappropriate**  
As cover for unaddressed misconception or prerequisite gaps; mark-trick theatre that abandons understanding; mastery claims from one mock paper.

**Learning intentions best served**  
Prepare for examination; Improve transfer (exam-surface transfer); Calibrate confidence; Increase procedural fluency (under time, when warranted).

**Typical episode types**  
Exam Application; Timed Practice; Mixed Review; Capstone; Confidence Calibration.

**Expected evidence**  
Improved timed performance relative to untimed baseline when knowledge is adequate; better time allocation; fewer misreads; calibrated readiness language.

**Advantages**  
Ecological validity for professional exams; reveals deployment deficits; complements conceptual work.

**Limitations**  
Dangerous if used early; anxiety load; easy to confuse with learning itself.

**Prerequisites**  
Sufficient conceptual and procedural warrant on the simulated objectives; honest diagnosis that exam technique — not absence of knowledge — is primary.

|--------------------|--------------------------|
| Repair misconception | Misconception Confrontation; Counterexample; Error-Led Teaching; Concept Comparison; Socratic Questioning |
| Build intuition | Direct Explanation; Analogy; Guided Discovery; Dual Representation; Worked Example |
| Strengthen prerequisite | Direct Explanation; Worked Example; Progressive Scaffolding; Deliberate Practice |
| Improve transfer | Interleaving; Concept Comparison; Dual Representation; Exam Simulation; Guided Discovery |
| Increase procedural fluency | Worked Example; Progressive Scaffolding; Faded Guidance; Deliberate Practice; Retrieval After Instruction |
| Consolidate understanding | Concept Comparison; Concept Mapping; Socratic Questioning; Retrieval After Instruction; Dual Representation |
| Recover confidence | Progressive Scaffolding; Think-Aloud Modelling; Socratic Questioning (gentle); Retrieval First (carefully) |
| Prepare for examination | Exam Simulation; Interleaving; Spaced Reinforcement; Think-Aloud Modelling; Deliberate Practice |
| Improve retention | Retrieval First; Retrieval After Instruction; Spaced Reinforcement |
| Connect fragmented knowledge | Concept Mapping; Concept Comparison; Interleaving; Dual Representation |

## 5. Summary Propositions

1. The catalogue names instructional approaches, not implementations.  
2. Each strategy defines fit, misfit, intentions, episode types, evidence, and limits.  
3. Affinity tables guide — they do not replace — reasoned selection.  
4. Specialised variants remain lawful when educationally justified and invariant-compliant.  
5. Composition of strategies across episodes is governed by the Composition Model, not by packing many strategies into one non-atomic episode.
